WESTERN EUROPE 


Non-Communist labor The intensified struggle between Com- 
munist and non-Communist labor in 
countries receiving ECA aid, particularly in France and Italy, 
indicates that the Communist labor organizations enjoy certain 
marked advantages in organizing the labor forces of these coun- 
tries. For the present, at least, they are able to draw upon 
greater reserves of money, “hard core” trained organizers, 
and newsprint and office supplies. These advantages are attri- 
butable in large measure to the demonstrated ability of the USSR 
to furnish both open and covert aid to those labor and political 
organizations which it regards as strategic instruments of its 
foreign policy. During the French coal strike, Soviet and Satel- 
lite trade unions openly raised and made available to the striking 
CGT miners steadily mounting sums in French francs. Besides 
such open aid channels, the USSR reportedly has had at its dis- 
posal the Banque Commerciale pour l’Europe du Nord, as well 
as outwardly legitimate commerical firms in France and Switzer - 
land through which it can siphon funds to Communist unions. In 
contrast, the non-Communist national labor organizations, such 
as the French Force Ouvriere and the Italian LCGIL, receive 
only limited financial assistance from the free trade union move- 
ments of the Western Hemisphere. This disparity between the 
foreign support received by the non-Communist and Communist 
labor forces in Western Europe has been an important factor in 
the retarded capabilities of the western-oriented trade unions 
for resistance to Communist-sponsored strikes and sabotage of 
production. Whenever the next major Communist blow at the 
European recovery program is launched, material assistance 
from Soviet and Satellite sources will be on an even larger scale.

INDIA -PAKISTAN 


Kashmir Peace Plan A peaceful solution of the Kashmir prob- 
lem at last appears likely, in view of the 
reported acceptance by both India and Pakistan of the 11 December 
proposals of the UN Commission for India and Pakistan (UNCIP). 
These proposals supplement the original UNCIP plan of 13 August 
by meeting Pakistan's bjections that the final disposition of Kashmir 
be in accordance with the wishes of Kashmir’s inhabitants. The new 
plan provides that the original cease-fire and truce arrangements 
be followed by a state-wide plebiscite, to be conducted under the 
direction of a UN plebiscite administrator under specified conditions 
designed to assure all Kashmiris of a free choice between accession 
to India and accession to Pakistan. Even though the means are now 
provided for a peaceful and permanent solution of the problem, con- 
siderable restraint will have to be exercised by both sides in the 
period which will elapse before the appointment of the UN adminis- 
trator and the holding of the plebiscite. Completion of the proposed 
plebiscite will probably require at least one year.
